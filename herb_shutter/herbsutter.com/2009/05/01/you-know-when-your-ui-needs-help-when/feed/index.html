]]> By: Igor Tandetnik https://herbsutter.com/2009/05/01/you-know-when-your-ui-needs-help-when/#comment-1183 Fri, 01 May 2009 11:56:07 +0000 http://herbsutter.wordpress.com/2009/05/01/you-know-when-your-ui-needs-help-when/#comment-1183 Plurals issue is more complicated than that. In many languages, it’s not just two forms for “one” and “many”. E.g. in Russian, there are three forms: one for when the number ends with 1 but not with 11, another for when it ends with 2, 3, or 4 but not 12, 13 or 14, and the third for everything else. And the three forms are not built uniformly for all nouns (like English day/days vs party/parties vs bus/buses vs scissors/scissors, but with even more variants and less regularity).

]]> By: Barry Kelly https://herbsutter.com/2009/05/01/you-know-when-your-ui-needs-help-when/#comment-1182 Fri, 01 May 2009 09:24:52 +0000 http://herbsutter.wordpress.com/2009/05/01/you-know-when-your-ui-needs-help-when/#comment-1182 I’m with you all the way up to the plurals issue (though here in Europe, both credit and debit cards use PINs).

On plurals, though, the “user-friendly” version is not good for localization. You need two separate localizable strings for singular and plural, as different words can get inflected depending on the language, or to switch things around so that you’re using a more symbolic notation, like ‘%d x %s’ or ‘%s: %d’.
